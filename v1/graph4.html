Chromogenic dyes & the colored coupler

In color negative film, three dye layers form during development — cyan, magenta, and yellow. Each layer starts from a colored coupler that has its own absorption before any dye has formed. how chromogenic dyes form
Color negative film has three emulsion layers, each sensitive to a different third of the visible spectrum. During development, exposed silver halide grains react with a developing agent that simultaneously oxidises and combines with a coupler molecule built into each layer. This coupling reaction forms a dye molecule in place — cyan in the red-sensitive layer, magenta in the green-sensitive layer, yellow in the blue-sensitive layer. The amount of dye formed is proportional to the amount of silver developed, which is proportional to the original exposure. More exposure → more dye formed.
what the slider shows — dye vs residual coupler
At 0% exposure (the film rebate, or the deepest shadow — no silver developed, no dye formed), the coupler is entirely unconsumed. Its own absorption dominates the spectrum. The pink coupler in the cyan layer absorbs strongly in the blue and green regions. The yellow coupler in the magenta layer absorbs in the blue region. At 100% exposure (maximum highlight), all coupler has been consumed and converted to dye — the residual coupler absorption disappears, replaced entirely by the dye's absorption curve. In between, both coexist: total absorption is the sum of dye plus remaining coupler.
why the orange mask exists
The film rebate — the clear border between frames — receives zero exposure. It contains no developed silver and no formed dye. But the coupler is still fully present. The pink residual coupler in the cyan layer absorbs blue and green light even with no dye present. The yellow residual coupler in the magenta layer adds further blue absorption. Together, these unconsumed couplers in the unexposed base produce a warm orange-yellow cast across the entire frame — the orange mask. It is not a defect or an accident; it is engineered deliberately to improve colour accuracy by partially compensating for the unwanted secondary absorptions of the cyan and magenta dyes themselves.
why the yellow layer needs no correction
The yellow dye absorbs only blue light, which is exactly what it should do. Its coupler compound is colorless — it contributes no absorption before the dye forms. This is why the yellow channel in a scanned negative is typically the cleanest: no mask contribution, no cross-channel contamination from the coupler. The cyan and magenta layers are where the complexity lives. The cyan dye in particular has a significant secondary absorption in the blue region — visible even at full exposure — which contributes to the colour correction problem that a 3×3 colour matrix must solve during inversion.
implication for scanning — what the scanner actually measures
When a scanner illuminates a frame of color negative, what each sensor channel receives is not a clean single-dye signal. The red channel receives cyan dye absorption plus the pink coupler residual. The green channel receives magenta dye plus yellow coupler residual. The blue channel receives yellow dye only — but also the combined residual absorption from both the pink and yellow couplers in the other layers, since all three layers are stacked. This is why the raw blue channel of a negative scan is typically the most suppressed — and why setting scanner exposure to balance the three channels before inversion is not straightforward. The correct exposure point differs per channel, and varies across the density range of the frame.
